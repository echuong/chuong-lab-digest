🔥 Weekly Highlights top 5

AI Summary

The standout preprint this week is TEsingle (Forcier et al., bioRxiv), a new tool for locus-specific TE expression in single-cell/single-nucleus RNA-seq that solves the multi-mapping problem for individual TE copies — directly relevant to the lab's efforts to track TE-derived isoforms at single-cell resolution. The tool reveals elevated LINE and ERV expression in Parkinson’s disease neurons and glia, reinforcing that TE reactivation marks disease states. Complementing this, Jo et al. (bioRxiv) identify 5,945 tissue-conserved m6A sites across 24 human tissues, with disruption of this stable m6A layer linked to cancer transcriptional changes — connecting to the lab’s METTL3/m6A work on TE regulation. Two m6A-virus papers (Zhang et al., PNAS on flaviviruses; Amarante et al., bioRxiv on Zika in mosquitoes) demonstrate METTL3 inhibition potently blocks viral replication, underscoring m6A as a conserved regulator of host-pathogen interactions.

In innate immunity, Li et al. (bioRxiv) show AIM2 undergoes liquid-liquid phase separation upon DNA binding to form inflammasome-activating condensates — notable because the lab’s MER41 work showed ERV-derived enhancers regulate AIM2 expression. The UCASpatial paper (Xu et al., Nature Communications) directly links HERV-H silencing with impaired type I IFN responses and a T cell-excluded microenvironment in human colorectal cancer, connecting ERV regulation to tumor immune evasion in a system parallel to the lab’s LTR10/CRC work. Among classics, Cheon et al. (Nature Structural & Molecular Biology) provide a systematic map of TE-gene chimeric transcripts using long-read sequencing and identify an RNA degradation-dependent quality control mechanism controlling TE-chimera expression — directly relevant to the lab’s TE exonization program. The ERVK18 paper (Wu et al., Clinical Cancer Research) demonstrates that locus-specific HERV-K expression predicts immunotherapy response in small cell lung cancer, reinforcing the translational potential of TE-based biomarkers the lab is pursuing with IFNAR2 splicing in ovarian cancer.
